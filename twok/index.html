Education Wednesday, March 18, 2026

East New York Families May Be Left Behind in the First Wave of 2-K

CB5 members raised concerns about District 19's absence from the city's initial 2-K rollout, uncertainty around Head Start after June 30, and a pay gap affecting community-based early-childhood programs.

Photo: Deric Johnson / East New York Times

At the March 5, 2026, Community Board 5 Education and Youth Services Committee meeting, members said East New York families could be left behind in the city's early-childhood rollout because District 19 was not included in the first wave of new 2-K seats and because there is still uncertainty about what will happen after June 30 to current Head Start arrangements.

The city announced on March 3 that the first phase of 2-K includes Districts 6, 10, 18/23, and 27 — not District 19. City Hall said the initial rollout will create more than 2,000 seats this fall, expand to 12,000 seats citywide by fall 2027, and reach full universality within four years.

District 19 Left Out of Phase One

During the meeting, one speaker said the city had "opened up more 2-K seats" but that the expansion "doesn't cover District 19." That concern aligns with the city's explanation of how the first districts were chosen, but the distinction matters: District 19 was left out of the first phase, not the entire program.

Speakers said that omission could have local consequences if East New York schools and providers are unable to compete for new seats. One participant said opportunities might be missed "if the district is not actively participating or saying anything." Another participant described how difficult it was to expand current pre-K offerings at a local school.

Head Start Uncertainty

The discussion then turned to programs already serving families in the neighborhood. "The contract is up," one speaker said, and another added, "We have no contract for next year," describing the uncertainty surrounding current Head Start programs and the fact that some centers still do not know what to expect next year.

City records show that children currently enrolled in NYCPS-contracted Head Start or Early Head Start programs may remain in their current programs through June 30, 2026, under city-funded transitional agreements.

Meeting attendees said that uncertainty is especially stressful because smaller Head Start and pre-K settings remain important for some children in East New York. Some suggested children are better served in classes of 10 to 14 than in classrooms of 28 to 30. Another speaker said small, independent Head Start sites are essential for working families, especially those needing overnight care arrangements.

A Two-Tier Pay System

Committee members and attendees also said community-based programs struggle to keep qualified teachers because Department of Education jobs pay more and offer better benefits. One speaker said the system has "two different unions and two different levels of pay," and another called it "not equitable."

Pay Gap — CBO vs. DOE Teachers (as of 2024–2025)

The NYC Comptroller reported in 2024 that contracts for non-union certified CBO teachers were still funded at October 2021 rates: $61,070 (bachelor's) and $68,652 (master's). The city estimated $41.6 million more per year would be needed to reach parity.

Role Bachelor's Degree Master's Degree
DOE Teacher (starting, Sept. 2025) $68,902 $77,455
CBO Certified Teacher (2021 contracted rate) $61,070 $68,652

No votes were taken on these issues at the meeting. But the discussion made clear that committee members see early-childhood access, program stability, and teacher retention as connected concerns for East New York residents.
