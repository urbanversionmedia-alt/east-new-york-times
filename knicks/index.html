The Knicks Brought $202 Million to New York. Will Any of It Reach East New York?

The Knicks are NBA champions for the first time in 53 years. East New York celebrated with the rest of this city, loud and all the way in. Now the question is whether the neighborhood gets its share of what comes next.

Deric Johnson Reporter, East New York Times 4 min read

The annual John P. White benefit basketball tournament at Grace Playground, East New York. Photo: Urban Version Media

The buzzer sounded just after midnight on Saturday. The New York Knicks defeated the San Antonio Spurs 94–90 in Game 5. Jalen Brunson scored 45 points. The city erupted. East New York erupted with it.

53 years is a long time. It is longer than most ENY residents have been alive. The last time the Knicks won, this neighborhood looked different. The country looked different. The game looked different. This championship belongs to a generation that grew up with the heartbreak and never got the payoff. Until Saturday night.

What It Means Here

Gersh Park at Linden Boulevard and Vermont Street has been hosting one of Brooklyn’s most competitive summer basketball tournaments for 18 years, Nike-sponsored for 13 of them. Council Member Charles Barron secured the funding to renovate it. Grace Playground hosts an annual benefit basketball tournament in honor of John P. White, a beloved local mentor and community member. The Prince Joshua Avitto Community Center on Schenck Avenue opened in 2018 with a college-sized basketball court built for this community. These are the courts where people in this neighborhood play. When the Knicks win, it lands here.

The $202 Million Question

Mayor Zohran Mamdani and the NYC Economic Development Corporation announced the Knicks’ playoff run generated an estimated $202 million in economic activity from home games. Each playoff game brought in roughly $90 million for the city. Bars and restaurants citywide reported nearly 50% increases in revenue on game nights.

The honest question is where that money actually went. Did any of it reach Pitkin Avenue, Fulton Street, or New Lots Avenue? Or did it stay concentrated around Madison Square Garden and Midtown while ENY residents watched the games at home? That question is worth asking, and the East New York Times will be following it.

Get to the Parade

Knicks Championship Parade — Thursday

  • Route: Canyon of Heroes, Lower Manhattan
  • From ENY: J or Z train to Fulton Street. Or take the A/C from Broadway Junction toward Manhattan.
  • Cost: Free
  • Tip: Leave early. Every train heading into Manhattan will be packed Thursday morning.

What the Neighborhood Deserves Next

A championship creates an opening. The Knicks and the city now have a moment to invest in the neighborhoods that make New York what it is. That means youth sports funding, court renovations, and real programming at places like the PJACC. It means a Knicks organization that shows up in East Brooklyn after the parade ends.

East New York is part of this city. It celebrated this title the same as everyone else. It deserves to be included in what the city builds off it. The East New York Times will keep asking whether that actually happens.

For now, go to the parade. Bring your kids. Take the train. Fifty-three years is a long time to wait, and East New York waited right along with the rest of New York.
