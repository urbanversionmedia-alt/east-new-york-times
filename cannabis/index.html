Unsupported Youth Marijuana Claim Shapes CB5 Cannabis Event Debate

Without a quorum, CB5's cannabis committee moved toward a summer fair after an unsupported claim about youth marijuana use, raising questions about evidence and influence in the planning.

By Deric Johnson · The East New York Times · June 2, 2026

CB5 Cannabis Management Review Committee meeting, East New York. Photo: Deric Johnson / The East New York Times

Community Board 5's Cannabis Management Review Committee informally moved toward a summer date for a proposed cannabis education fair after a tense exchange over whether the event should be tied to October's national substance-use prevention month — a debate that included an unsupported claim that young people are not misusing marijuana when school resumes.

The moment raised concerns about the committee's decision-making process: an unevidenced claim about when young people misuse cannabis appeared to help move the discussion away from October, even though federal public-health agencies recognize October as a national prevention month and youth cannabis use is tracked as an ongoing public-health issue.

The committee did not have a quorum at the time the informal consensus formed, meaning no binding vote was taken. Nevertheless, the discussion moved the planning process toward a summer timeline without a formal decision-making record or evidence-based justification for the change.

What the Claim Said — and What the Data Shows

During the meeting, a committee member suggested that youth marijuana use is not a concern when school begins, implying that timing the event outside of October — the federally designated National Substance Use Prevention Month — would be equally effective for community education. No source or data was cited to support this claim.

Federal data from the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention consistently show that youth cannabis use does not follow a seasonal pattern tied strictly to the school calendar. The CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Survey tracks cannabis use as a year-round concern among adolescents, with no evidence that summer months represent a lower-risk window.

Questions About Process

The exchange raised broader questions about how CB5's cannabis committee makes decisions and what standards of evidence apply when shaping community programming. Critics of the committee's direction have previously raised concerns about the relationship between the committee and the cannabis industry operators it is tasked with reviewing.

The East New York Times will continue to follow CB5's cannabis committee as it plans its proposed education event and monitor how community input is incorporated into the final decision.
